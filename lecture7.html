Types of power plants

  • Nuclear power plant - very high initial investment, low operational costs, provisions for decommission, radioactive wastes
  • Coal power plant - dirty energy source, long-term contracts with coal suppliers, it is necessary to cover production with $CO_2$ certificates
  • Gas power plant - cleaner energy source, fast start-up which can cover peaks of demand, not so expensive to build
  • Hydro power plant - cheapiest energy source, can produce only certain amount of energy proportional to its reservoir volume, should produce only when price is highest
  • Fuel oil power plant - the most expensive to run, turned on only in extreme price spikes
  • Renewable energy sources (solar, wind, thermal, biomass) - subsidized and with guaranteed acccess to the grid, high production swings coming from changing weather conditions

Merit order

  • All producers receive one price for their production.
  • Only variable costs (=fuel in our simplification) matter, fixed/investment costs do not enter economic decision making
  • Producers with low variable costs produce always, producers with high variable costs produce only if price is high enough to justify running the factory
  • Merit order (supply stack) - chain of generation sources sorted by their variable costs
  • Market price is then given by the cost of the last producing unit in merit order
  • Typical stack is following: hydro $\Rightarrow$ nuclear $\Rightarrow$ coal $\Rightarrow$ gas $\Rightarrow$ oil
  • The biggest obstacle is hydro - near-zero marginal cost but profit should be maximized by inter-temporal balancing
  • Other issues: spacial aspects, outages, power plants are not binary, export/import as external variable

Basic dispatch

  • Producer of a single unit $i$ wants to maximazi profit $\Omega_i$ which equals $\pi P_i - C_i(P_i)$ where $P_i$ is the power produced (during an hour), $\pi$ is the energy price and $C_i(P_i)$ is the cost of producing this amount of energy.
  • Maximum is reached when $d\Omega/dP_i=0$ which is when
    • \begin{equation} \frac{d(\pi P_i)}{dP_i}=\frac{dC_i(P_i)}{dP_i} \end{equation}
  • The first term is marginal revenue, the second marginal cost.
  • When $\pi$ is a market price, it doesn't depend on $P_i$ and we receive
    • \begin{equation} \frac{dC_i(P_i)}{dP_i}=\pi \end{equation}
  • Producer should increase input until his marginal costs equal market price.

Other dispatch considerations

  • Every power plant has maximum and minimum output limits which constrain possible profits.
  • No-load cost - quasi-fixed costs which the power plant incures while it is connected to the system (independent of power generated)
  • Start-up cost - cost of getting the power plant running (quasi-fixed). These costs must be amortized over longer time periods. Even negative profit for some hours is better than incurring this cost again.
  • Excessive start-ups and shut-downs damage the power plant $\rightarrow$ minimum uptime and downtime constrains.
  • For reasons above, power plant cannot be optimized from hour to hour and price forecasts need to be made.

Heat rate, thermal efficiency and spreads

  • Thermal efficiency is a ratio of electricity produced to energy consumed (thermal plants 30-50%)
  • Heat rate is energy input (in MMBtu) required to produce 1 MWh of electricity.
  • Both heat rate and thermal efficiency are power plant specific.
  • If we assume that variable production cost $C_i(P_i)$ consists solely of fuel which we burn, then we obtain spread valuation models.
  • Power plant can be looked at as an option on spread with strike equal to variable non-fuel costs (usually assumed zero).
  • Binary dispatch - either the spread is positive and we produce at full capacity or negative and we don't produce at all.
    • \begin{equation} Spark ~ spread = Power ~ price - Heat ~ rate ~ * ~ Fuel ~ price \end{equation}

Shortcoming of spark spread (and other spreads)

  • Using spark spread valuation could lead to overvaluation of generation assets due to:
    • Unrealistic assumtions used for price modelling.
    • Primitive dispatch - issues as noted before: minimum uptime and downtime, start-up costs, limited number of starts, ramp-up costs
    • Operational problems - supply shortage, dispatch coordination, bargaining pressure of suppliers
      • Most popular spreads are:
        • Nuclear - quark spread
        • Coal - dark spread
        • Gas - spark spread
