Chemistry

  • Hydrocarbons = {natural gas, crude oil, coal}
  • Hydrocarbons are made up of the elements hydrogen and carbon, plus impurities
  • The lower the number of carbon molecules, the lighter the compound
  • # of carbon molecules: gas $<$ crude oil $<$ coal
  • Laws of physics
    • increased pressure: gas $\Rightarrow$ liquid
    • increased temperature: liquid $\Rightarrow$ gas
  • Natural gas - hydrocarbons in the gas phase at standard temperature and pressure (room temperature, atmospheric pressure): methane ($C_1H_4$), ethane ($C_2H_6$), propane ($C_3H_8$), butane ($C_4H_{10}$)
  • Sometimes $C_{5+}$ components (pentane-hexadecane) added to the definition because they could be gases in the reservoir

Natural gas components

  • Methane - main component of natural gas (usually 70%-90%)
    • dry gas - more than 95% of methane
    • wet gas - less than 95% of methane
  • LNG (liquefied natural gas) - liquid product produced by cooling methane to -161.5 degrees Celsius
  • LPG (liquefied petroleum gas) - propane and butane in pressurized containers
  • NGL (natural gas liquids) - ethane, propane, butane, $C_{5+}$
  • The heavier hydrocarbon, the more carbon molecules, the more heat generated, the higher price premium to the price of pure methane

Impurities and ecology

  • Nonhydrocarbon components - carbon dioxide ($CO_2$), hydrogen sulfide ($H_2S$), hydrogen, nitrogen, helium, argon
  • $CO_2$ and $H_2S$ must be removed before transport and sale - they can corrode pipelines and are air polutants, $CO_2$ is a green house gas
    • sour gas - high level of $H_2S$
    • sweet gas - low level of $H_2S$
  • inert gases nitrogen, helium and argon are neither corrosive nor toxic, but their presence reduces overall heat energy contained in the gas
  • Natural gas is the cleanest burning primary fossil fuel, burning methan releases only $CO_2$ and water.

Natural gas storage

  • Gas storage facilities are buffer between supply and demand because production is constant but demand is cyclical.
  • Excess supply is injected in summer into storage and withdrawn during winter.
  • Two uses:
    • Long-term base load storage - meets seasonal demand increases. Gas is injected during low-demand months and withdrawn during high-demand season.
    • Short-term peak load storage - designed to have high deliverability for short periods of time, covers daily and intra-day swing.
  • Pipeline could be used as a peak load storage.
  • Low injections in the summer provide an indication that demand is high relative to production and that the industry may enter the heating season with a low inventory buffer $\Rightarrow$ prices should go up
  • High levels of natural gas in inventory close to the end of winter send strong bearish signal because market will be flooded with unusually high supply.

Storage characteristics

  • The higher the pressure in the storage facility, the more readily the gas may be extracted.
  • Cushion gas - gas which must remain in storage because it cannot be recovered (due to equal pressures at the wellhead).
  • Working gas - gas which is stored and withdrawn on each cycle.
  • Deliveribility - measure of the amount of gas which can be withdrawn on a daily basis.
Types of storages:
  • Depleted gas reservoirs - cushion gas and infrastructure already in place, cheapest to operate and maintain
  • Aquifers - rock formations which used to be water reservoirs, expensive to develop, high cushion gas requirements (up to 80%)
  • Salt caverns - high deliverability and minimum leakage, lowest cushion gas requirements (1/3)

Gas hubs

  • Hub - central place for trading activity where supply and demand meet, either physical delivery or virtual trading
  • Main hubs in Europe:
    • NBP (GB)
    • TTF (NL)
    • NCG, GPL (DE)
    • ZTP (BE)
    • PEGN, TRS (FR)
    • CEGH (AT)
    • PSV (IT)
  • Majority of trades is concluded on NBP or TTF.
  • Very high correlation between hubs $\rightarrow$ Western/Central Europe is a single integrated market area with good connectivity

Gas producers in Europe

  • Key players are Russia (Gazprom) and Norway (Statoil) with combined market share over 50 % (other main producers are Algeria and Netherlands)
  • Both companies tried/try to influence the price by supply management which keeps prices below costs of new gas suppliers of US LNG but high enough to maximize rent
  • Higher liquidity in hubs forces supplier to switch from indexed oil formulas (with high premiums) to hub based evaluation.
  • Imports of LNG shall grow. Azerbaijan is about to enter market in 2019.
