Terminology

  • Crude oil - unrefined petroleum which exists in liquid phase in natural underground reservoirs
  • Refining - process of purification of crude oil
  • Major refined products are:
    • Gasoline - used for cars
    • Diesel - used for cars
    • Kerosene - jet engines, tractors
    • LPG (propane, butane) - fuel, petrochemical input
    • Naphtha - intermediate that can be further processed to make gasoline
    • Residual fuels, coke, asphalt, benzene, toluene, xylene, petrochemicals, lubricating oil....

Crude oil characteristics

  • There are many types of crude oil
  • Roughly 160 different grades of crude oil are traded internationally
  • Two most qualities are:
    • Viscosity (thickness, density) - lighter crudes are more valuable as they produce more valuable refined products, measured in API gravity, oil with API gravity below 10 is heavier than water and vise versa
    • Sulfur content - the greater the sulfur content, the more costly the refining process
      • to remove it, sour crudes - high sulfur content, sweet crudes - low surfur content
  • Two most important oil benchmarks (prices of other oils are calculated by adding a negotiated differential to the benchmark price) are (both are light and sweet):
    • WTI (West Texas intermediate) - API gravity 39.6 degrees, 0.24% sulfur
    • Brent Blend (the North Sea, UK) - API gravity 38.3 degrees, 0.37% sulfur

Refineries

  • Every refinery is different in the sense of technological flexibility
  • Producing higher-margin products costs more as more advanced technology needs to be used
  • The mix of products is driven by:
    • Refinery complexity - ability to process crude feedstocks into value-added products, expressed in Nelson Complexity Index, crude distillation has factor of 1.0, very complex refineries have NCI over 9
    • Location - location determines how well the refinerey can access feedstocks and distribute its product, if products cannot be moved to market, there is no value in refining them
    • Product demand - market demand has huge impact on refinery profitability, demographics plays a key role, motor fuel preferences can change

Refining value and crack spreads

  • Refiners' profits depend on the spread (difference) between the price of crude oil and the prices of refined products $\Rightarrow$ crack spread
  • Crack spread represents the theoretical refining margin and is quoted in dollars per barrel.
  • Crack spread must take local preferences into account:
    • US - 3:2:1 spread because it uses ratio of 3 crude to 2 gasoline plus 1 heating oil
    • Europe - 6:2:3:1 spread (6 crudes, 2 gasoline, 3 diesel, 1 residual)
  • Positive crack spread $\Rightarrow$ value of outpus is higher than value of inputs $\Rightarrow$ positive gross cracking margin

    • Q: 3:2:1 crack spread when crude USD 38.00/barrel, gasoline USD 1.00/gallon and heating oil USD 0.90/gallon?

    A: USD 2.6 = (1.00 x 42 x 2 + 0.90 x 42 - 3 x 38)/3

Production of oil

  • The primary driver of cost is the nature and geography of the reservoir
  • Oil industry is a sector (as with other commodities) where competition is primarily cost-based, companies cannot obtain competetive advantage by differentiation
  • There are 3 possible stages of oil recovery:
    • Primary recovery - uses natural reservoir pressure
    • Secondary recovery - external fluid or gas is injected into reservoir in order to increase reservoir pressure
    • Tertiary recovery - try to change chemical conditions of crude (by altering viscosity or adhensive tensions) - thermal recovery, chemical flooding, gas injection
  • Both secondary and tertiary recovery are extremely expensive and can be justified only by stable high oil prices
