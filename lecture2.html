Terminology

  • Spot price - price for 'immediate' delivery
  • Forward - contract on future delivery for a fixed price, OTC deal
  • Futures - contract on future delivery for a fixed price, exchange deal
  • Option - contract on possible future delivery for a fixed price where the buyer of the contract can decide if he wants to execute or not
  • Swap - Contract on exchange of payments

Power contracts

  • Spot - immediate delivery is organised by the market operator (state company) which ensures balance of supply and demand
  • Forward/Futures - Power is delivered over standardized periods - years (Y17), quarters (Y16Q3), months (Y16M02), weeks (Y16W08), weekends (Y16WE09) or days (Y16M02D23)
    • Two main types of delivery:
    • Baseload - 24/7
    • Peakload - every working day 8-20
  • Option - outright options are scarse and only over the counter, cross-border transmission capacities have option-like characteritics

Power contracts - examples

Q: You bought 5 MW of Y17 baseload product for 26.5 EUR/MWh. How much do you have to pay for the whole delivery?
A: 1,160,700 EUR (5*365*24*26.5)

Q: The same product is quoted at 24.5 EUR/MWh after a while. What is MTM (mark-to-market) of your deal?
A: - 87,600 EUR (5*365*24*-2)

Q: Y16Q2 has price 29 EUR/MWh, Y16M04 has price 29.5 EUR/MWh and Y16M05 has price 29 EUR/MWh. How much does Y16M06 have to cost so that there is no arbitrage opportunity (=possibility of riskless profit with no upfront payment)? Assume a month has 30 days...
A: 28.5 EUR/MWh (29.5+29+28.5=3*29)

Why forwards?

  • Speculations - You can speculate on commodity price movement without owning it. Open a position and close it before delivery.
  • Hedging - Producers can fix the price of their future production and thus be insulated from market price volatility.

Cross-hedge - when the underlying of the contract is not perfectly correlated with the commodity you hedge (for example hedging jet fuel with crude oil). This gives rise to basis risk.

Strip hedge - we hedge each individual obligation with a futures contract.

Stack hedge - all future exposure is hedged by a single maturity futures contract, usually the nearest-term product. Stack and roll strategy (basis risk is present).

Why hedge?

Reasons to hedge:
  • Taxes
  • Bankruptcy and distress costs
  • External financing
  • Increase debt capacity
  • Managerial risk aversion
Reasons not to hedge:
  • Transaction costs
  • Costly expertise
  • Managerial controls in place
  • Complex reporting

How to hedge - hedge ratio

When you hedge commodity with forward on another commodity (basis risk), how many forwards do you need? Notation:
  • $S_t$ is the price of commodity which you want to hedge
  • $F_{t,X}$ is the forward price on another commodity with maturity X
  • $\sigma_S$ is volatility of $\Delta S$
  • $\sigma_F$ is volatility of $\Delta F$
  • $\rho$ is correlation between $\Delta S$ and $\Delta F$
  • h is hedge ratio, number of forwards to hedge 1 commodity unit
Principle - minimize the variance of your hedged position: \begin{equation} h=\rho \frac{\sigma_S}{\sigma_F} \end{equation}

How to hedge - hedge ratio II

The idea of hedge ratio: You need commodity at time T. You buy h forwards now, $t=0$, and at time T you sell forwards and buy commodity on spot. Your total price for commodity is: \begin{equation} S_T+h*F_{0,X}-h*F_{T,X} \end{equation} You minimize the variance of this equation by choosing h as hedge ratio (there is a proof in Hull, Chapter 3).

Note: h is the regression coefficient of $\Delta S$ on $\Delta F$.

Forward curve

Forward curve - a collection of futures ordered by maturity Basic shapes:
  • Backwardation - downward sloping curve, spot $>$ forward
  • Contango - upward sloping curve, spot $ <$ forward
Normal backwardation theory - natural shorts (producers) dominate the market over natural longs (industrial company). Speculator fill the gap and demand compensation (=risk premium P) for their risk-taking. \begin{equation} F_{0,T}=E_0[S(T)]-P \end{equation}

J. M. Keynes: risk premium is positive (generally not necessary...)

Cross-border transmission capacity (CBTC)

  • The right to transfer power from one country to another.
  • CBTC is granted by the market operator in auction.
  • CBTC should be executed only if it makes economic sense, i.e. if you profit from the execution.
    • \begin{equation} execution ~ profit=max(0,P_{sink}-P_{source}) \end{equation}
  • CBTC can be modelled as an option on country spread

Q: You bought 5 MW $CZ>DE$ (from CZ to DE) CBTC for Y16M03D01 and Y16M03D02 for 2 EUR/MWh. What is your total profit if prices in those days were 25(28) EUR/MWh and 23(19) EUR/MWh for CZ(DE), respectively?
A: -120 EUR (-2*5*48+3*5*24+0*5*24)

Option basics

  • Long position - option was bought, short position - option was sold
  • Call option - right to buy, payoff max(0, S-K); put option - right to sell, payoff max(0, K-S)
  • Intrinsic value - payoff if we could immediately exercise the option
  • Time value - value inherent in our right (not obligation) to exercise the option
    • \[ \text{Option value}=\text{Intrinsic value}+\text{Time value} \]
  • European style option - payoff at maturity, American style option - execution possible prior to maturity
  • Vanilla option - European or American, exotic option - fancier rules (Bermudan, Asian (average price), barrier (price must hit a barrier before execution), binary (all-or-nothing nature))

Greeks

  • Greeks represent sensitivities of option value to changes in underlying parameters
  • Delta $\Delta$ - sensitivity to underlying spot price $S$
    • \[ \Delta = \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} \]
  • Vega $\nu$ - sensitivity to volatility
    • \[ \nu = \frac{\partial V}{\partial \sigma} \]
  • Theta $\Theta$ - sensitivity to time decay
    • \[ \Theta = \frac{\partial V}{\partial t} \]
  • Gamma $\Gamma$ - sensitivity of Delta to spot price
    • \[ \Gamma = \frac{\partial \Delta}{\partial S}=\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} \]

Delta

  • Option value locally behaves as if we held position in $\Delta$ underlying.
  • Delta of call option in $[0,1]$.
  • Delta of put option in $[-1,0]$.
  • For put and call with same underlying, maturity and strike:
    • \[ \Delta_{call} - \Delta_{put} = 1 \]
  • Deep ITM option: $\Delta=\pm 1$, deep OTM option: $\Delta=0$
  • Put-call parity:
    • \[ C-P=D(F-K) \] D - discount factor, F - forward price

Gamma

  • Gamma measures curvature of option value with respect to underlying.
  • Always positive for long positions and negative for short positions.
  • Gamma is highest for at-the-money options.
  • Second term in Taylor expansion with respect to price of underlying.
