Definitions

  • Credit risk - possibility that you will lose money when your counterparty is in default
  • Credit exposure - the total amount of money which you can lose in case of counterparty default
  • Credit rating - assessment of counterparty's solvency
  • Probability of default - probability that counterparty will default in a given time period (usually a year)
  • Settlement risk - the risk of not being paid after you make delivery
  • Replacement risk - the risk of losing positively-valued (with positive MTM) contract
  • MTM (=mark to market, M2M) - revaluation of our contract with current prices
\begin{equation} Credit ~ risk = Settlement ~ risk + Replacement ~ risk \end{equation}

Settlement risk

  • Settlement risk is connected with physical delivery of energy.
  • Invoices are usually sent and paid after delivery is made (settlement specifics are always part of the contract)
  • In some markets (especially electricity) it is difficult to stop delivery immediately when your counterparty misses the due date.
  • For example if the due date is the 20-th after the delivery month (standard payment scheme), then settlement exposure is a 3-month delivery worth (accounting for delays in reporting, notice letters, market operator notification and actual disconnection).
Q: What is the settlement exposure if we are in the middle of selling 5 MW baseload for 23 EUR/MWh and standard payment scheme is used?
A: 248,400 EUR (=23*5*24*3*30)

Replacement risk

  • Replacement risk is connected to MTM of our contract.
  • If $MTM>0$, then our contract contains an economic value which we can lose if our counterparty defaults
  • MTM exposure is calculated from future delivery only, that is energy we need to buy/sell for worse price if contract defaults.
  • Contrary to settlement risk replacement risk can arise from both selling and buying.

Credit risk of portfolio of deals

  • If deals with one counterparty are done separately, then only positive exposures are taken into account and all are added $\rightarrow$ the worst case, any new deal can only increase exposure
  • Trading is usually done under EFET (European Federation of Energy Traders) agreement - standardized template for a bilateral umbrella agreement.
  • There are two types of netting depending on local jurisdiction:
    • Close out netting - the best case, the court recognizes economic value of contracts and allows settlement-replacement netting
      • \begin{equation} Exposure = \sum_{portfolio}Settl_i + \sum_{portfolio}Repl_i - Collaterals \end{equation}
    • Payment netting - only payments can be netted
      • \begin{equation} Exposure = \max(0,\sum_{portfolio}Settl_i) + \sum_{portfolio}\max(0,Repl_i) - Collaterals \end{equation}

Credit rating

  • Magnitude of credit exposure should be always proportional to counterparty's solvency
  • Counterparty's ability to keep its commitments is reflected in its rating.
  • Credit rating is estimation of creditworthiness and is usually expressed as a letter or letters.
  • Possible rating scale is (S&P scale): AAA, AA, A, BBB, BB, B, CCC, CC, C, D where triple A is the best rating and D means counterparty in default.
  • Every rating is connected to a certain probability of default and a credit limit should be assigned to it.

Mitigating credit risk

Credit risk can be mitigated several ways:
  • Adhere to risk limits.
  • Received bank guarantee can cover exposure (at least partially).
  • Prepayments or more favourable billing scheme can reduce settlement risk.
  • Mother company can provide PCG (parent company guarantee) for its child.
  • Cash can be deposited as a collateral.
  • Use of credit rating or balance sheet triggers in the contract.
